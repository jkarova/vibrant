Summary

This course introduces students to geometry as used in design applications, especially computer graphics. It does so with a mixture of lecture, readings and lab practicums that trace these applications from pre-historic roots of geometry to the present.

In the practicums students use hand-drawing and computer modeling to execute visual problems in constructive geometry, surface symmetries, trigonometry, projection, spline and surface geometry.


Objectives

Students will gain a practical understanding of the following:

  1. The principles of classic constructive geometry, i.e., Euclidean geometry, and the application of these principles to solving geometric problems.
  2. A working knowledge, gained in a common graphic environment, of the representation of classic geometry in vector space.
  3. The principles of projective geometry as they apply to 3D projections and realizing these principles using constructive means, especially angle intercept and the graphic concept of reverse perspective.
  4. Pre-calculus intuitions of conic and spline curves including curved surfaces.
  5. A "tradesman's" knowledge of trigonometric relationships.


Structure

The course is structured around a series of themes that roughly parallels the historic developments of mathematical applications in art, while offering a progressive growth in geometric intuitions and computer presentation. There are extensive hands-on assignments, which comprise the laboratory component of the course. There will also be weekly quizzes ­ open book and open notes. Careful note-taking is a must.
